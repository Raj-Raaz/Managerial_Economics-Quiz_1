Theory of Production

Production Functions

Key Symbols: \( q \) or \( Q \) = Total Output, \( L \) = Labor (workers), \( K \) = Capital (machinery), \( A \) = Total Factor Productivity (technology level).

Cobb-Douglas

Formula: \( q = A L^p K^q \)

Where \( p \) and \( q \) are output elasticities of Labor and Capital. Returns to scale are determined by \( p + q \). If \( p + q = 1 \) (constant), \( > 1 \) (increasing), \( < 1 \) (decreasing).

Linear Function

Formula: \( q = aL + bK \)

Inputs are perfect substitutes. Marginal products are constant. Here, \( a \) is the output per unit of labor, and \( b \) is the output per unit of capital.

Marginal & Average Product

  • Marginal Product of Labor (\( MP_L \)): The extra output produced by employing one more unit of labor holding other inputs constant. Calculated via partial derivative: \( \frac{\partial q}{\partial L} \).
  • Average Product of Labor (\( AP_L \)): Total output divided by total input (\( q / L \)). It tells you the average contribution per worker.

The Critical Relationship:

  • 1. When \( MP > AP \): The Marginal Product pulls the Average Product up (AP is increasing).
  • 2. When \( MP < AP \): The Marginal Product pulls the Average Product down (AP is decreasing).
  • 3. When \( MP = AP \): The Average Product is at its absolute maximum point (where the curves intersect).

Short-Run Cost Functions

You will frequently be given a Total Cost (TC) function, representing the total expense to produce a given quantity (\( q \)). E.g., \( TC = 300 + 50q + q^2 \). You must derive the following:

Cost Metric Meaning / Formula Example: \( TC = 300 + 50q + q^2 \)
Fixed Cost (FC) Costs that do not change with output. The constant term independent of \( q \). \( 300 \)
Variable Cost (VC) Costs that change as output increases. All terms containing \( q \). \( 50q + q^2 \)
Average Variable Cost (AVC) Variable cost per unit produced.
\( \frac{VC}{q} \)		 \( \frac{50q + q^2}{q} = 50 + q \)
Average Fixed Cost (AFC) Fixed cost spread over output.
\( \frac{FC}{q} \)		 \( \frac{300}{q} \)
Marginal Cost (MC) The cost of producing one additional unit. Derivative of TC with respect to q: \( \frac{d(TC)}{dq} \) \( 50 + 2q \)

Cost Minimization Strategy

The core rule for minimizing costs (finding the optimal input combination of Labor and Capital) is equalizing the "bang for the buck" across inputs.

\( \frac{MP_L}{w} = \frac{MP_K}{r} \) Equal Marginal Product per Dollar
\( MRTS = \frac{w}{r} \) Marginal Rate of Technical Substitution
Definitions:
  • \( MP_L \) & \( MP_K \): Marginal Product of Labor & Capital.
  • \( w \): Wage rate (the price of hiring one unit of Labor).
  • \( r \): Rental rate (the price of hiring one unit of Capital).
  • MRTS: The rate at which a firm can substitute Capital for Labor while keeping output constant (\( MP_L / MP_K \)).

Algorithm to solve minimization problems:

  1. Calculate \( MP_L \) and \( MP_K \) by taking the derivative of the production function.
  2. Set up the optimality condition: \( \frac{MP_L}{MP_K} = \frac{w}{r} \).
  3. Solve the equation to find one variable in terms of the other (e.g., you might find \( K = 2L \)).
  4. Substitute this relationship back into the target production function (e.g., \( Q = ... \)) or cost budget to find the exact numerical values for optimal Labor (\( L^* \)) and Capital (\( K^* \)).

Consumer Preferences & Budgets

Axioms of Preference

  • Completeness: Consumers can always compare and rank bundles (A > B, B > A, or A = B). If a consumer is confused and unable to decide, completeness is violated.
  • Reflexivity: Any bundle is at least as good as itself (A ≥ A). Rarely tested directly, but appears as a distractor option.
  • Transitivity: Preferences are logical and consistent. If Bundle A is preferred to B, and B is preferred to C, then A MUST be preferred to C. A circular ranking (A>B>C>A) violates this.
  • Convexity: Consumers prefer variety. Averages or mixtures of bundles are preferred to extreme, single-item bundles.

Utility & Marginal Utility

For a utility function \( U(x,y) \), the marginal utility of a good is its partial derivative — the extra satisfaction from one more unit of that good, holding the other constant.

\( MU_x = \dfrac{\partial U}{\partial x} \)    \( MU_y = \dfrac{\partial U}{\partial y} \)

"More is better" (monotonicity): both \( MU_x > 0 \) and \( MU_y > 0 \).

Diminishing marginal utility of a good means its own marginal utility falls as consumption of that good rises — check whether \( MU_x \) still contains \( x \) (and falls as \( x\) rises), not whether it depends on the other good.

The Budget Line

\( I = P_xX + P_yY \)

Where \( I \) = Income, \( P_x, P_y \) = Prices of goods X & Y, and \( X, Y \) = Quantities consumed.

Scenario shifts to remember:
• Income changes: If income (\( I \)) halves, intercepts halve, but slope remains the same (parallel inward shift).
• Price changes: If price of X (\( P_x \)) drops, the budget line pivots outward on the X-axis (as shown above), creating a flatter slope.

Opportunity, Implicit & Sunk Costs

Explicit Costs

Actual out-of-pocket money payments requiring an accounting transaction. (e.g., leasing computer time, buying gas, paying grocers, car insurance).

Implicit Costs (Opportunity Cost)

The value of the next best alternative given up by using resources owned by the firm. No actual cash changes hands. (e.g., Your foregone wage at a part-time job if you start a business, or foregone interest on invested capital).

Sunk Costs

Costs that have already been incurred and cannot be recovered under any circumstances. Economic theory dictates they should be completely ignored in forward-looking decisions.

The "Fritos" Scenario Question Pattern:

Scenario: You bought merchandise for $100. The grocer buys returns at $0.25 on the dollar (so $25 is recoverable). A local frat offers to buy it from you for $55.

  • Opportunity Cost to sell to Frat: What do you give up by not returning it to the store? You give up the $25 refund. (Opp Cost = $25).
  • Decision: Since the Frat's offer ($55) > Your Opportunity Cost ($25), Yes, you should sell it to the frat.
  • Sunk Cost: Historical cost - Recoverable cost = $100 - $25 = $75. (This $75 is gone no matter what you do).
