🏭 Production Functions

💡
Definition: A technological relationship showing the maximum output \( q \) produced from inputs capital \( K \) and labour \( L \): \( q = f(K, L) \).

① Cobb-Douglas

\( q = A K^\alpha L^\beta \)

  • Smooth, convex isoquants.
  • Exhibits any returns to scale (depends on \( \alpha+\beta \)).
  • Both MPL and MPK diminish as input increases.

② Leontief (Fixed Proportions)

\( q = \min(aK, bL) \)

  • L-shaped isoquants ("Perfect complements").
  • Inputs must be used in a fixed ratio.
  • Example: "Exactly 2 workers per machine."

③ Linear (Perfect Substitutes)

\( q = aK + bL \)

  • Straight-line isoquants.
  • Constant MRTS. One input can replace another at a fixed rate.
  • Example: "Trade 3 labour for 1 capital, output constant."

📊 Marginal Product & MRTS

\( MP_L = \frac{\partial q}{\partial L} \) (Extra output from +1 Labour, holding K constant) \( MP_K = \frac{\partial q}{\partial K} \) (Extra output from +1 Capital, holding L constant) \( MRTS_{L \text{ for } K} = \frac{MP_L}{MP_K} \) (Slope of isoquant)
⚠️
Exam Trap: Always check "other inputs held constant." If the question says "allowing other inputs to vary," that is WRONG. Marginal product strictly requires ceteris paribus.

The Relationship Between Marginal and Average

  • If \( MP_L > AP_L \), \( AP_L \) is rising.
  • If \( MP_L < AP_L \), \( AP_L \) is falling.
  • If \( MP_L = AP_L \), \( AP_L \) is at its maximum.

📈 Returns to Scale

What happens to output when all inputs are scaled by factor \( t \)?

  • Constant (CRS): Output scales by exactly \( t \).
  • Increasing (IRS): Output scales by more than \( t \).
  • Decreasing (DRS): Output scales by less than \( t \).
For Cobb-Douglas \( q = A K^\alpha L^\beta \): scale by \( t \rightarrow q' = A(tK)^\alpha (tL)^\beta = t^{\alpha+\beta}q \). Returns to scale depends entirely on \( \alpha+\beta \).

💰 Cost Minimization (Isoquants & Isocosts)

Isoquant

Combinations of \( L \) and \( K \) that produce the same output \( q \).
Slope = \( -MRTS \).

Isocost Line

Combinations of \( L \) and \( K \) with the same total cost: \( C = wL + rK \).
Slope = \( -w/r \).

Optimal Condition (Tangency): Slope of Isoquant = Slope of Isocost \( MRTS = \frac{MP_L}{MP_K} = \frac{w}{r} \) Corner Solution (Linear PF): If \( MRTS > w/r \), use ONLY Labour. If \( MRTS < w/r \), use ONLY Capital.
Figure: Cost Minimization occurs where Isocost is tangent to Isoquant.

🏷️ Cost Theory (Short Run vs Long Run)

\( TC = FC + VC \) \( MC = \frac{dTC}{dq} = \frac{dVC}{dq} \) \( AFC = \frac{FC}{q} \) | \( AVC = \frac{VC}{q} \) | \( ATC = \frac{TC}{q} \)
🎯
The "Marginal Pulls the Average" Rule:
If \( MC < ATC \), ATC is falling.
If \( MC > ATC \), ATC is rising.
If \( MC = ATC \), ATC is at its MINIMUM. (Same logic applies to MC and AVC).

Short Run vs Long Run

  • Short Run: At least one input is fixed (usually Capital \( K \)). Hence, we have Fixed Costs.
  • Long Run: All inputs are variable. No fixed costs. \( TC = LTC \). The Long Run Average Cost (LRAC) curve is an envelope of Short Run Average Cost (SRAC) curves.
  • Economies of Scale: When LRAC falls as output increases. (Due to specialization, bulk buying).
  • Diseconomies of Scale: When LRAC rises as output increases. (Due to managerial inefficiencies).

⚖️ Budget Line & Consumer Theory

Equation: \( P_x \cdot x + P_y \cdot y = I \) Slope \( = -\frac{P_x}{P_y} \) X-intercept \( = \frac{I}{P_x} \) | Y-intercept \( = \frac{I}{P_y} \)
  • Price Change: Alters the slope and one intercept. (Pivot of the line).
  • Income Change: Alters both intercepts but slope remains same. (Parallel shift).

🧠 Preferences & Utility

Axioms of Rational Preferences:

  • Completeness: Consumer can compare any two bundles. (Violation: "confused, unable to compare")
  • Transitivity: If A>B and B>C, then A>C. (Violation: "Mango>Apple, Apple>Guava, Guava>Mango")
  • Convexity: Averages preferred to extremes. Implies diminishing MRS. Leontief (perfect complements) is not strictly convex.
  • Monotonicity: More is better. (Utility increases with quantity).

Marginal Utility (MU)

\( MU_x = \frac{\partial U}{\partial x} \). At consumer optimum: \( \frac{MU_x}{P_x} = \frac{MU_y}{P_y} \).

🔄 Opportunity & Sunk Cost

💡
Opportunity Cost: Value of the next-best alternative foregone.
Sunk Cost: Unrecoverable past cost (ignore in forward-looking decisions).
Explicit Cost: Out-of-pocket cash payments.
Implicit Cost: Foregone value of self-owned resources (e.g., foregone salary).

Economic Profit = Total Revenue - Explicit Costs - Implicit Costs.
Accounting Profit = Total Revenue - Explicit Costs.

How to use this: Memorize the formulas and the "Pattern Recognition" table. Before submitting any exam, run through the "Exam Traps" section to ensure you haven't fallen for standard trick questions.

🧮 Core Formulas

  • Production: \( q = f(K,L) \)
  • MPL: \( \partial q / \partial L \)
  • MPK: \( \partial q / \partial K \)
  • MRTS: \( MP_L / MP_K \)
  • Cost Min: \( MP_L / MP_K = w / r \)
  • TC: \( FC + VC \)
  • MC: \( dTC/dq \)
  • ATC: \( TC/q \)
  • AVC: \( VC/q \)
  • AFC: \( FC/q \)
  • Budget Slope: \( -P_x / P_y \)
  • X-Intercept: \( I / P_x \)
  • Consumer Optimum: \( MU_x / P_x = MU_y / P_y \)

🚨 Common Exam Traps

  • "MPL with other inputs allowed to vary": WRONG. Marginal product strictly holds other inputs constant.
  • "Cobb-Douglas has constant MPL/MPK": WRONG. Both diminish.
  • Confusing AFC with AVC: AFC always falls continuously. AVC is U-shaped.
  • MC intersects ATC at minimum: Not maximum. Minimum!
  • Forgetting corner solutions: For Linear PF \( q = aK + bL \), you almost never use both inputs. Compare MRTS to w/r and pick one.
  • Opportunity Cost vs Sunk Cost: Opportunity cost is what you give up *now* (next best alternative). Sunk cost is unrecoverable and must be ignored.
  • Budget Line Slope Sign: Always negative.
  • "Elmo has convex preferences?": No. Perfect complements (Leontief) are weakly convex, not strictly convex.

⏱️ Exam Day Tips

  • No negative marking: Never leave a question blank. Guess if you must!
  • Read "Average" vs "Marginal" carefully: The wording is the biggest trick in cost theory questions.
  • Reverse Engineering Cost Functions: If given AFC, MC, AVC at specific quantities, assume \( TC = a + bq + cq^2 \).
    - \( FC = AFC \times q \)
    - \( MC = b + 2cq \)
    - \( AVC = b + cq \)
    Solve using simultaneous equations.
  • Cost Minimization Steps: 1) Find MRTS. 2) Compare to w/r. 3) If equal, use tangency. 4) If unequal, use corner solution (0 for one input). 5) Plug into production function to find exact L* and K*.
